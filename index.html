Welcome

I am a Ph.D. Student in Agricultural and Applied Economics at Virginia Tech's Department of Agricultural and Applied Economics, where I work under the supervision of Dr. Bradford Mills and Dr. Anubhab Gupta.

My research focuses on development economics, climate change economics, and gender and labor economics in agricultural contexts. Specifically, I examine how climate variability and extreme weather events affect agricultural productivity and farmer decision-making in Sub-Saharan Africa, with a particular focus on the Senegal Groundnut Basin.

My doctoral research investigates the economic impacts of extreme heat on groundnut production, land access dynamics, and youth engagement in agriculture. I employ econometric methods, remote sensing techniques, and spatial analysis to understand how environmental and economic factors shape agricultural outcomes and rural livelihoods.

Before joining Virginia Tech, I earned my Agricultural Engineering Degree from École Nationale Supérieure d'Agriculture de Thiès in Senegal, where I developed a strong foundation in agricultural systems and rural development.

I aim to contribute to evidence-based policy solutions that enhance agricultural resilience and promote sustainable development in climate-vulnerable regions. My goal is to pursue a career in academic research or international development, where I can continue investigating the intersection of climate change, agriculture, and economic development.

Research

Research Interests

My research agenda centers on understanding the economic dimensions of climate change in agricultural systems, with particular emphasis on:

  • Climate Change Economics: Quantifying the economic costs of extreme weather events on agricultural productivity
  • Development Economics: Examining land access, resource allocation, and productivity in developing countries
  • Gender and Labor Economics: Analyzing gender disparities and youth engagement in agricultural production
  • Applied Econometrics and Remote Sensing: Using satellite data and spatial analysis to measure agricultural outcomes

Teaching

Teaching Philosophy

My approach to teaching is grounded in clarity, engagement, and application. I believe that economics is best learned through evidence and examples that connect theory to the challenges people face in their daily lives. I aim to create a classroom environment that encourages critical thinking, collaboration, and independent reasoning.

My teaching would combine lectures with discussions, applied exercises, and the use of real data to help students link economic models to empirical realities. I also value inclusivity and ensure that each student's background and perspective enrich classroom learning.
